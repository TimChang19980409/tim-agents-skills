# Concept Progression

## When to use

Use when the user needs to explain a programming, system design, or architecture concept by building a logical path from concrete intuition to abstraction and transfer.

## Inputs

- Concept or architecture topic
- Audience background and misconceptions
- Desired transfer target, such as reading code, making a design decision, or explaining tradeoffs
- Source material or domain constraints

## Steps

1. Identify the familiar anchor: a concrete problem, symptom, or decision the learner recognizes.
2. Sequence the concept as observation, naming, rule, example, variation, and transfer.
3. Add contrasting cases to separate similar ideas.
4. Include a misconception repair step with replacement language.
5. Delegate to a domain skill if exact architecture, framework, or runtime behavior is central.

## Safety gates

- Do not start with abstract taxonomy unless the audience is already advanced.
- Do not combine too many neighboring concepts in one progression.
- Do not make architecture tradeoffs look universal when they depend on context.

## Outputs

- A concept ladder
- Concrete examples and contrasting cases
- Misconception repair notes
- Transfer exercise or decision prompt
- Technical verification notes when needed

## Verification

- Check that each step answers "why now?" for the learner.
- Confirm the learner has a chance to apply or distinguish the concept.
- Explicitly identify any domain detail that requires specialist verification.
